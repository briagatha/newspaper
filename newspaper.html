Birth of a Nation

By: Brianna Williams

America's first full length film, the movie involved to families, one from the South, and one from the North. It took place around the Civil war when the South fought against the North so they could keep Slavery legal. The first family was the Stonemans of the North and the Camerons from the South. The Stoneman brothers visit the Cameron family, the oldest brother of them, Phil, falls in love with Margaret Cameron while Ben Cameron idolizes a picture of Phil's sister Elsie Stoneman. Then when the Civil begins all the men join their sides of the armies.

Later on a Black Militia ransacks the Cameron home but the women are rescued by soldiers. Meanwhile, the younger Stoneman and two of the Cameron brothers are killed in the war. Ben Cameron is found wounded after saving someone in which he receives the nickname "The little Soldier" and is taken to a Northern hospital. Where he gets to meet Elsie Stoneman who is working as a nurse there at the hospital. Ben has the opportunity to leave the hospital. At home he receives news that Elsie has been tricked and captured. In fact captured by the Black Militia leader who ransacked the Cameron home. Ben goes and saves her, They later on become married, as well as Phil and Margaret.

This movie was filmed in 1914, when the film was released in 1915, it shattered both box office and film-length records, running three hours and ten minutes. In 1998, it was voted #44 in the "Top 100 American films" by the American Film Institute. The movie was based off of Thomas Dixon Jr.'s novels, such as The Clansman and The Leopard's spots. But the film was charged for racism and protests by the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP). And because most of the actors where white, the film was banned by few review boards. The Birth of a Nation had been credited as groundbreaking for its innovative application of the medium of film.

According to the film historian Kevin Brownlow, he states that the film was "Astounding in its time" and that "so many advances in filmmaking technique that it was rendered obsolete within a few years." The work has received widespread criticism for it blatantly racist, of scenes that are presented onscreen as a documentary. Film Critic Roger Elbert writes, "Certainly The Birth of a Nation presents a challenge for Modern Audiences." People who are uninterested in film history or don't understand silent movies, they probably won't be interested in this film. But it definitely is an important part of history.
